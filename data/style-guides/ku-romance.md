# Style Guide: Mass-Appeal Kindle Unlimited Romance Fiction

---

## Who This Is For

This style sheet is intended for use by an LLM writing commercial romance fiction for the Kindle Unlimited market. The reader is trope-aware, reads fast, reads in volume, and will disengage within two chapters if chemistry and interiority do not land. The prose should feel personal and immediate, like reading a friend's detailed retelling of something that happened to her. Every stylistic choice should serve the push-pull between emotional vulnerability and emotional defense.

All instructions below are written as directives. Follow them.

---

## 0. Techniques Inherited From the Author's Native Voice

The author's thriller manuscript (Oracle Veil) does not translate to romance wholesale, its emotional register is restrained and masculine, its pacing is built for violence, not intimacy. But six specific craft techniques in that manuscript are genre-agnostic and should be preserved here. Each is cited from the source so the instruction is concrete, not abstract.

**1. Unmarked free indirect thought.** The source never tags internal thought with "he thought" or "she realized." It just drops the thought into the narration:

> "These weren't terrorists."
> "Now his project became his life and livelihood."

Romance application: keep doing this for the protagonist's running interpretation of events. It is the single biggest lever for close POV and it is already a native habit. Do not regress to tagging thoughts.

**2. Italics reserved for the sharpest, most unguarded line only.** The source doesn't italicize every internal thought, it saves italics for the one or two lines per scene that are too raw or too honest for the character to say out loud, often short and often profane:

> *I hate him.*
> *Gods, I fucking hate him.*
> *Why me? Why the fuck is it always my fault?*

Romance application: this is a stronger tool for romance than for thriller, because romance lives on the gap between what the character will admit and what she actually feels. Reserve italics for the line that breaks through her deflection. If every thought is italicized, none of them land. One per scene, maybe two.

**3. The single-sentence paragraph as a hard stop.** The source uses isolated one-line paragraphs constantly to land a beat before moving on:

> "He didn't answer."
> "Moussa stiffened."
> "Darkness."
> "Bruno sat down."

This is already covered as a rule in Section 7 below, but it's worth naming here as a native habit rather than an imported rule. It's the author's strongest existing instinct and should be leaned on harder in romance, where the stakes are emotional instead of physical, but the mechanism (isolate the moment, let it sit) is identical.

**4. Repetition with escalation.** The source uses exact phrase repetition with an intensifying detail to build dread:

> "Fifteen times. He'd watched Noah die fifteen times. And come back fifteen times."
> "Forty-eight times. He'd watched Noah die forty-eight times. And come back forty-eight times."

Romance application: this transfers directly to longing and tension counting. "Third time he'd caught her looking. Third time she'd looked away." Use sparingly, it loses power if it becomes a tic.

**5. The deflating, dry internal aside under pressure.** The source consistently undercuts a serious moment with a flat, ironic internal line instead of playing the moment straight:

> "It was also the most ironic in this situation."
> "Thank God."
> "Just like Bangkok."

This is the closest thing to romance's wit-as-armor convention that already exists in the author's voice. It should be the primary source of banter in this style guide rather than inventing a separate comedic register from scratch. The wit in Section 4 (tone) should sound like this, dry, fast, slightly self-mocking, not like sitcom banter.

**6. Counting and ticking-clock structure for real-time emotional escalation.** The source uses literal timestamps (00:00, 00:06, 00:38) to jump between characters' heads during a single unfolding event, and elsewhere uses literal counting ("One. Two. Three.") to mechanize a moment of crisis.

Romance application: this is a genuinely strong device for a high-tension single scene with dual POV, a wedding entrance, a confession, the seconds after a kiss breaks. It will read as a deliberate craft choice, not a gimmick, if used once or twice in the manuscript at true peak moments, not as a default structure. Do not use it for ordinary scenes. It is a flare, not a habit.

**What does NOT transfer:** the source's emotional restraint, its sparse and infrequent direct emotion language, its violence-paced sentence rhythm, and its near-total avoidance of sustained interiority on feeling (it dwells on tactical thought, not emotional thought) are all wrong for romance and are deliberately overridden in the sections below. Sections 5 and 6 ask for more dwelling and more emotional vocabulary in internal thought than the source ever uses. That is intentional, not an oversight, romance requires it and thriller actively avoids it.

## 1. Narrative Rhythm

**Summary:** Alternate between short, charged sentence clusters during tension and longer sensory sentences during softer emotional moments. Pacing is never uniform. Use rhythm to signal emotional temperature.

**Key traits:**

- During charged scenes (near-kisses, arguments, first realizations), drop to short sentences and fragments. Maximum five words each. Let the whitespace work.
  > "He was watching her. She felt it before she looked. She looked."

- During emotional processing and internal dwelling, allow medium to long sentences with subordinate clauses that circle back on themselves, because this is how the character actually thinks.
  > "She'd told herself it didn't mean anything, that she was reading into a look that probably meant nothing, that men like him probably looked at every woman that way, and none of that made her hands stop shaking."

- Quiet scenes have longer breath. Charged scenes shorten to close to staccato. The rhythm should make the reader feel the shift before they consciously register what is happening.

- Chapter endings should leave an emotional question open, not always a plot question. The reader should feel unresolved. "She didn't sleep" is more effective than "Something had changed."

---

## 2. Close vs Distant POV

**POV distance:** Write in very close, deep POV. The narrative lives inside the protagonist's perception, filtered entirely through what she notices, how she interprets it, and what she does not let herself admit. There is almost no narrative distance and no authorial commentary.

**Evidence:**

- The love interest is never described objectively. Describe him only through her physical response to him. Not "he was tall and broad-shouldered" but "she had to tip her chin up to look at him, which she resented."
- Internal contradiction is the engine of the story. The protagonist should observe one thing and interpret it through a defensive filter. "He smiled. It was the kind of smile that meant nothing. She was sure of it."
- Render internal thoughts in the narration without framing devices. No "she thought" or italics for every internal line. Let thought and narration blur. The reader should not always be certain where observation ends and interpretation begins.
- What she notices about him tells us about her, not about him. Filter every description through her emotional state. A detail she notices when she is angry about him reads differently than the same detail noticed when she is off-guard.

---

## 3. Formality

**Formality level:** Informal.

**Indicators:**

- Contractions everywhere, including in narration. "She didn't know" not "She did not know." Exceptions only when a character is being deliberately formal.
- Colloquial sentence structure in both narration and internal thought. Sentence fragments and run-ons are not errors, they are voice.
- Avoid formal connective phrases: "however," "therefore," "consequently." Replace with casual connectives or a new sentence.
- The protagonist's internal voice and the narrative voice should be nearly indistinguishable. The reader should feel like they are inside her head, not being told about her.

---

## 4. Overall Tone

**Core tone adjectives:** Warm, wry, slightly self-deprecating, emotionally honest.

**Tone stability:** The tone stays fundamentally warm even in conflict. The protagonist has a wry internal voice that deflects emotion with light humor, but the humor is a defense mechanism, not the actual emotional register. When the defense breaks, the prose goes soft and direct. This contrast is the engine.

- The protagonist should be self-aware enough to notice when she is rationalizing, and tell the reader about it without calling it by name: "It was a very reasonable thing to feel. She told herself that four times."
- The love interest reads as slightly warmer and less controlled than he appears on the surface. His warmth should leak through the cracks, not be announced.
- Avoid sustained ironic distance. The story earns its emotional moments by being sincere about them when they arrive.

---

## 5. Emotional Range

**Range description:** Wide. The protagonist moves through defensiveness, irritation, longing, embarrassment, quiet pleasure, and grief within a single chapter. The transitions should feel real rather than performed.

**Common emotions and how to render them:**

- **Longing:** Rendered as absence. She notices the space where he isn't. She tracks him without meaning to. She edits herself in conversation with him.
- **Irritation (at him, at herself):** Short sentences, physical displacement. She puts things down too hard. She leaves rooms.
- **Embarrassment:** The specific detail she cannot stop thinking about. Not "she was embarrassed" but "she was still thinking about the exact face she had made at 2 in the morning."
- **Quiet pleasure:** Slow down. Longer sentences. Let the moment breathe. Name the sensory detail, not the emotion.
- **Grief or old wound:** Brief. Surface and retreat. The character does not dwell on backstory in real-time. She notices it and moves on. The reader should feel what she is avoiding.

---

## 6. Average Sentence Length and Rhythm

**Sentence length:** Short to medium, with deliberate variation. Average is seven to twelve words in narration. Internal thought goes shorter. Description goes longer.

**Rhythmic patterns:**

- Use a long sentence to establish, then a short sentence to land it:
  > "She'd spent twenty minutes choosing what to wear to something that was not a date, for a person who had given her no indication he thought about her at all. She wore the blue dress."

- Use sentence fragments for physical response and emotional reaction. Never explain the fragment. Let it sit.
  > "His hand on her back. Just for a second. Just to guide her through the door. She felt it for the rest of the night."

- Avoid three or more sentences of the same length in a row. Uniformity of sentence length reads as generated and drains the momentum.

- Avoid stacking three participial openers in a row ("Turning to him, noticing the way he..."). No more than one participial opener per paragraph.

---

## 7. Paragraphing

**Paragraph length:** Short to very short. One to three sentences as the standard. Single-sentence paragraphs are normal and frequent. Use them for landing emotional beats, for physical reaction, and for what she decides not to say.

**Paragraph function:**

- Each paragraph advances something: her attention, her interpretation, a physical detail, the tension level. No paragraph should exist as neutral connective tissue.
- Use a single-sentence paragraph after a significant moment to let it land before moving on:
  > She nodded.
  >
  > He didn't look away.
  >
  > Neither did she.
- Break for impact at the point of physical awareness. The moment she notices something about him gets its own paragraph.
- Long paragraphs signal reflection and internal processing. Short paragraphs signal action, tension, or charged stillness. Use the paragraph break as a pacing tool.

---

## 8. Average Grade Level

**Estimated grade level:** 5th to 6th grade.

**Complexity factors:**

- Vocabulary stays accessible. Where a character uses a specific technical or professional term (tied to their job or world), use it once and let context clarify. Do not define it.
- Sentence structure stays simple to compound. Avoid heavily subordinated clauses in action or dialogue.
- The emotional complexity of the story lives in what the character notices and avoids, not in the vocabulary used to describe it. The prose can be simple while the psychology is layered.
- Reading should feel effortless. If a sentence requires a second read, it probably needs to be split.

---

## 9. Dialogue Style

**Voice and realism:** Dialogue feels natural and slightly unfinished. Characters interrupt each other, trail off, and answer questions with different questions. Subtext carries more weight than text. What is not said should be audible.

**Tag and beat usage:**

- Use "said" and "asked" as default tags. Avoid "replied," "inquired," "responded," and all adverb-tagged dialogue ("she said softly," "he said firmly"). Replace with a specific action beat instead.
- Action beats over dialogue tags wherever possible. The beat reveals character and controls pacing.
  > "I don't need your help."
  > He tilted his head. "You've been staring at the same page for twenty minutes."
- Banter should have a volley rhythm. Short lines, fast turns, with an undertow of awareness that neither character acknowledges.
- Dialogue between the protagonist and love interest should feel like a negotiation. Each character is testing the other. Each line reveals something they didn't intend to reveal.
- Characters should have distinct speech patterns. The love interest's dialogue should be identifiable without a tag. Give him a specific habit: directness, deflection with a question, understatement, or something he never says outright.
- Avoid on-the-nose emotion in dialogue. Characters do not say "I was scared" or "That meant a lot to me." They say the adjacent thing and let the reader feel what is underneath.

---

## 10. Sentence Openings

**Common opening patterns:** Character name or pronoun, sensory anchor, short declarative, or the thing she is avoiding. Openings should vary and should not announce what the sentence is doing.

**Variety vs repetition:**

- Avoid starting more than two consecutive sentences with "She" or "He." Vary the opening by leading with the sensory detail, the action, or the object.
- Do not open paragraphs with "It was," "There was," or "There were." These constructions distance the reader from the POV character's experience.
- Sentence-opening variety should be invisible. The reader should not notice it; they should only feel that the prose moves naturally.
- One effective opener type: begin with the thing she notices before she names who or what she noticed it about. Creates immediacy and slight disorientation.
  > "The chair across from her was empty when she arrived. By the time she looked up from her phone, it wasn't."

---

## 11. Clause Structure and Complexity

**Typical clause types:** Simple and compound dominant. Complex sentences appear during reflection and internal monologue. Avoid compounding subordination in action or dialogue sections.

**Stacking vs splitting:**

- In tension or action, split ideas into separate sentences. Do not stack multiple clauses onto a single sentence when the moment is charged. Splitting creates urgency.
- In internal processing, stacking is appropriate. The character thinks in run-ons when she is circling something uncomfortable. Let the clauses pile up when her thinking is not yet resolved.
  > "She didn't know when she had started tracking him across rooms, or whether he noticed, or whether she wanted him to, or whether that was worse."

**Subordination patterns:**

- Avoid heavy subordination ("although," "whereas," "in spite of the fact that") in narration. These read as authorial distance.
- "Because" is acceptable when it is the character's own rationalization: "She was annoyed. Because she had a right to be. Because he had no business looking at her like that in front of everyone."

---

## 12. Punctuation Habits (No Em Dashes)

**Core punctuation tools:**

- Commas for rhythm and natural pause within a sentence.
- Periods to cut and land.
- Ellipses, sparingly, for trailing thought or trailing dialogue where the character stops herself: "She almost said what she meant. Almost."
- Question marks in internal thought to signal the character questioning herself.
- Colons to set up a list or a landing statement, no more than once per scene.

**Constraints and guidance:**

- Em dashes must NOT be used when imitating this style.
- Use a period and a new sentence instead of an em dash for dramatic interruption.
- Use a comma instead of an em dash for parenthetical insertion.
- For interrupted dialogue, end the line in a period and open the next line with the interrupting character. Do not use a dash to signal the cut.
  > "I don't think you understand what I."
  > "I understand exactly what you meant."
  The period creates a harder stop and reads more natural in fast exchange.

---

## 13. Emphasis and Cadence Tricks

**Emphasis techniques:** Emphasis is created through position, brevity, and the paragraph break, not through punctuation or formatting. Place the most important word or phrase at the end of a sentence. Isolate the crucial moment in its own single-sentence paragraph.

**Rhythmic patterns:**

- Long sentence building to short landing: earns the short sentence's weight.
- Repetition with shift: a phrase repeated twice with a variation on the third creates a cadence that lands without announcing itself.
  > "She knew what it meant. She knew what she should do. She did neither."
- The physical observation that lands after a beat of non-acknowledgment. Notice something, skip past it, return to it two sentences later. Mirrors how attention actually works under emotional pressure.

**Signature moves:**

- The body's knowledge before the mind catches up: she reacts physically and registers it cognitively one or two beats later.
- The deflection followed by the admission: character insists on the rational interpretation, and then the next paragraph quietly contradicts it without comment.
- The catalogued detail: in a significant moment, the protagonist notices one very specific, slightly absurd detail rather than processing the moment as a whole. This grounds the reader and reads as authentic.
  > "He said her name. She was thinking about the fact that his jacket had a small thread loose at the collar. She kept thinking about it after he was gone."
- Short chapter ending that does not explain itself:
  > "She didn't call him.
  >
  > She thought about it for three days."

## Summarized Style Rules (Checklist)

### Do:

- Write in deep, close POV. The narrator and the protagonist are the same voice.
- Describe the love interest through her physical and emotional response only. Never objectively.
- Use short paragraphs and single-sentence paragraphs to control pacing and land emotional beats.
- Keep the average sentence short. Vary it. Short in tension. Medium in narration. Longer in her internal reflection.
- Use action beats in place of dialogue tags. Make every beat reveal something about character or subtext.
- Let the protagonist notice a very specific, concrete detail during charged moments instead of narrating the feeling itself.
- Write internal contradiction as a feature. She is wrong about herself. The reader should see it before she does.
- Use the paragraph break as a pacing tool. Break at the moment of physical awareness or emotional shift.
- Keep grade level at 5th to 6th. Accessible vocabulary. Emotional complexity lives in psychology, not diction.
- Let scenes breathe occasionally. A quiet, low-stakes scene earns the charged ones that follow. Not every scene escalates.
- Give the love interest distinct speech: something he says or doesn't say that belongs only to him.
- End chapters on emotional open questions, not always plot ones.
- Use repetition with variation for emphasis and cadence. Three-beat structures that shift on the third.
- Have the protagonist's body know things before her mind catches up.

### Avoid:

- Em dashes anywhere. Replace with a period and new sentence, or a comma.
- Over-explained emotion. "She felt angry because he had dismissed her" becomes a physical action and a paragraph break.
- Pre-loading significance: "the moment that would change everything." Let the moment be what it is. The reader will feel its weight without being told.
- Uniform sentence length across a paragraph or scene. Monotony kills momentum.
- Three or more consecutive sentences beginning with "She" or "He."
- "She couldn't help but" — have her do the thing.
- Stacking three participial openers in sequence ("Turning, noticing, feeling...").
- On-the-nose emotion dialogue: "I'm scared of how you make me feel." Write what's adjacent.
- Melodramatic clichés: "her heart hammered in her chest," "her blood ran cold," "time stood still," "the silence was deafening." Replace with a specific physiological detail.
- "In that moment," "without warning," "suddenly." These are authorial intrusions. Let the scene speak.
- Thesaurus cycling on character references. She is "she" and "her." He is "he" and "him" and his name. Not "the infuriating man" or "the dark-haired architect."
- Beige connective passages: her crossing rooms, making coffee, navigating spaces with no sensory or emotional content. Either cut or give the action a second job.
- "Not just X but also Y." Direct statement only.
- All characters speaking in the same register. The love interest's dialogue should be identifiable without a tag within three scenes.
- Letting the protagonist fully resolve her feelings within a scene. Partial resolution only. The reader should finish a chapter with something left unfinished.
