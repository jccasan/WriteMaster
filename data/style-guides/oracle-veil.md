# Style Guide — Oracle Veil

## 1. Narrative Rhythm
- **Summary:** Write in short, punchy bursts. The default sentence is brief. Long sentences are the exception, earned by sensory accumulation or deliberate pacing — not by default. The rhythm creates momentum even in static scenes.
- **Key traits:**
  - Open paragraphs with short declarative sentences, then let detail accumulate: "Magnambougou soccer field was dark, uneven, and too far from anything official. No lights. No uniforms. Just four men standing apart from each other like they'd been told not to look like a group."
  - Use standalone fragment paragraphs to land emphasis: "They paid well. Too well." / "Cash. All in advance." / "Darkness." / "Fair trade."
  - Hard cuts between scenes. No transitional paragraphs. One scene ends, the next begins.
  - In action, go one beat per sentence: "A gunshot tears through the cockpit. The bullet exiting the windshield just centimeters from Moussa's head."
  - Paragraphs are short — 1 to 3 sentences is the norm. Single-sentence paragraphs appear constantly and should be treated as a primary tool, not an exception.

## 2. Close vs Distant POV
- **POV distance:** Very close. The narration fuses with the viewpoint character's perception at all times. There is almost no authorial distance or commentary. The reader knows only what the character knows, sees only what they see, and hears their internal voice woven directly into the narration.
- **Evidence:** Internal thoughts are rendered in italics, but are not framed with tags like "he thought" or "she realized." They appear as italicized narration that bleeds seamlessly into the surrounding prose. Example: *I hate him.* / *Not a charter. Once he takes off, he can't land.* / *This would be so much easier if I could just kill him.* The italics mark the thought as distinct from narration, but the absence of tags keeps the reader inside the character's head without distance.

## 3. Formality
- **Formality level:** Informal.
- **Indicators:** Contractions in narration ("She's ugly, only a parent could love her"). Contemporary internal monologue voice regardless of historical setting (*Why the fuck is it always my fault?*). Military jargon and proper nouns used precisely and without over-explanation. Characters speak in clipped, direct exchanges with no social pleasantries.

## 4. Overall Tone
- **Core tone adjectives:** Grounded, terse, darkly wry, world-weary, controlled.
  - Grounded: Extraordinary events are treated as operational facts. "Noah didn't even feel them; all he could do for a split second was admire the marksmanship."
  - Terse: No lingering, no over-explanation. "Go. Nothing has changed."
  - Darkly wry: Irony surfaces in internal monologue and situation framing. "A cemetery. Of course, it's a cemetery."
  - World-weary: Characters have seen too much and feel it. Tomas's internal voice is sardonic and superior; Noah's is restrained and controlled; Miranda's is raw and contemporary.
  - Controlled: Emotion is suppressed in action; it surfaces in what characters do, not what they say.
- **Tone stability:** Shifts register by POV. Tomas is sardonic, Noah is formal-restrained, Miranda is raw and reactive, Martin is measured. Hold each character's register consistently within their sections.

## 5. Emotional Range
- **Range description:** Wide emotional range rendered through action and physical response, never through direct statement. Characters feel everything; they name nothing. The range moves from detached professionalism to grief to dark humor to awe without announcement.
- **Common emotions:** Grief, controlled fear, dark irony, professional detachment, loyalty. All shown through behavior: Miranda performs CPR she knows is pointless. Danforth vomits after calling the naval strike. Martin keeps his hands folded so they won't shake.

## 6. Average Sentence Length and Rhythm
- **Sentence length:** Short. The majority of sentences are under 12 words. Longer sentences appear for sensory scene-setting or deliberate build, and are followed by short ones to land the rhythm.
- **Rhythmic patterns:**
  - Cluster short sentences in action: "One grabbed his pistol. One reached for Moussa. One tried to get into the co-pilot seat."
  - Use medium sentences for world-building: "The air above the Oran approaches was a sheet of sound — HMS Rodney's big guns hammering somewhere out in the haze, Vichy batteries on Djebel Santon answering back."
  - Return to short after any extended passage.
  - Occasional single-word or two-word sentences for maximum punch: "Darkness." / "He froze."

## 7. Paragraphing
- **Paragraph length:** Short. One to three sentences in most cases. Action sequences sometimes go one sentence per paragraph.
- **Paragraph function:** Each paragraph holds one beat — one action, one thought, one revelation. Do not combine beats into long paragraphs. Use white space as punctuation. Dialogue beats always get their own paragraphs.

## 8. Average Grade Level
- **Estimated grade level:** 6th grade.
- **Complexity factors:** Vocabulary is accessible. Sentence structure is predominantly simple (subject-verb-object). Complexity comes from situation and subtext, not from syntax. Specialist vocabulary (military terminology, foreign-language insertions) is used precisely and without over-explanation — define nothing, trust the reader.

## 9. Dialogue Style
- **Voice and realism:** Terse, purposeful. Characters rarely speak more than two sentences without interruption or action. No character explains things they would both already know. Subtext is the dominant mode — characters speak past each other, withhold, and deflect.
- **Tag and beat usage:** Use "said" and "asked" as default tags. Prefer action beats over tags: "Danforth said. The words tasted like iron." Avoid adverb-tagged dialogue ("she said angrily"). Let action carry emotion.

## 10. Sentence Openings
- **Common opening patterns:** Character name or pronoun ("He," "She," "Moussa," "Tomas"). Time or location markers ("Ten minutes later," "Behind him"). Brief action verbs to open paragraphs directly.
- **Variety vs repetition:** "He" and "She" openers repeat frequently — vary deliberately. Open with action, location, or dialogue when repetition builds.
- **Distinctive habits:** Foreign-language insertions appear at moments of character authenticity ("Ilū iparrasūni," "Jemrwal," "Je t'aime, mon amour") — preserve this, it defines character voice. The timestamp structure (00:00, 00:06, 07:55) for multi-POV real-time scenes is a signature structural device — use it in scenes where multiple characters experience the same moment from different vantage points.

## 11. Clause Structure and Complexity
- **Typical clause types:** Simple sentences dominate. Compound sentences appear for cause-and-effect. Complex subordinate clauses are rare and deliberate.
- **Stacking vs splitting:** Always split. If an idea can be two sentences, make it two sentences. "He walked the helicopter. He ran his hand along the fuselage, checking panels and switches he already knew were correct. Bought time."
- **Subordination patterns:** Minimal. When "because" or "until" appears, it's purposeful and brief.

## 12. Punctuation Habits
- **Core punctuation tools:** Periods do the heavy lifting. Commas are minimal — where another writer would use a comma to join clauses, this author uses a period. Question marks appear frequently in internal monologue. Ellipses appear occasionally for trailing thought.
- **Constraints:** Em dashes must NOT be used. Use a period or a new sentence instead. Where another writer would use an em dash for interruption or parenthetical, split into two sentences or use a comma.

## 13. Emphasis and Cadence Tricks
- **Emphasis techniques:** Single-sentence or single-word paragraphs. Direct fragment repetition for emotional hammering: "NOT AGAIN! NOT AGAIN!" / "Forty-eight times. He'd watched Noah die forty-eight times. / And come back forty-eight times." Rhetorical questions in italicized internal monologue that go unanswered: *Is that even possible? How bad was it?*
- **Rhythmic patterns:** Build detail in triplets of short sentences, then cut to one word or one short sentence. "One grabbed his pistol. One reached for Moussa. One tried to get into the co-pilot seat. / A gunshot tears through the cockpit."
- **Signature moves:**
  1. Timestamp POV structure for real-time multi-character scenes (00:00, 00:06, etc.)
  2. Standalone fragment paragraphs as punctuation: "Fair trade." / "Darkness." / "It has begun."
  3. Internal thoughts rendered in italics without framing tags — no "he thought," no "she realized." The italics do the work.
  4. Tense shift to present inside past-tense scenes for immediacy: "A gunshot tears through the cockpit" mid-past-tense action
  5. Characters named or addressed in italicized mid-thought without framing: *Gods, I fucking hate him.*

## Summarized Style Rules

**Do:**
- Write short. Default sentence is under 12 words. Earn longer sentences.
- Give each beat its own paragraph.
- Italicize internal thoughts. Use no framing tags ("he thought," "she realized") — the italics carry it.
- Use single-sentence and single-word paragraphs for emphasis.
- Use action beats instead of dialogue tags wherever possible.
- Hard-cut between scenes. No transitional paragraphs.
- Trust the reader on specialist vocabulary. Define nothing.
- Split clauses into separate sentences. Always.
- Match internal voice register to character: Tomas is sardonic, Noah is formal-controlled, Miranda is raw and reactive, Martin is measured.
- Use the timestamp structure for multi-POV real-time scenes.
- Let emotion surface through what characters do, not what they feel.
- Use present tense inside past-tense narration for punch in action beats.
- Preserve foreign-language insertions at moments of character authenticity.

**Avoid:**
- Em dashes in any form.
- Labeled emotions ("she felt scared," "he was nervous").
- Framing tags on internal thoughts ("he thought," "she realized," "she wondered").
- Dialogue tags beyond "said" and "asked."
- Long expository paragraphs.
- Transitional or bridging prose between scenes.
- Adverb-modified dialogue tags ("he said quietly").
- Explaining foreign-language insertions.
- Elevated or formal vocabulary unless required by historical POV.
- Complex subordinate clause structures.
- Combining multiple beats into a single paragraph.
- Authorial commentary or narrative distance.
