HOOK RUBRIC — WHAT MAKES A STORY PITCH COMMERCIALLY VIABLE AND EMOTIONALLY COMPELLING

Every pitch must satisfy ALL criteria below. A pitch that fails even one criterion is not ready.

---

## THE FIVE HOOK REQUIREMENTS

### 1. A Protagonist with a Clear Want AND a Hidden Need
The Want is the external, conscious goal — what the character is actively chasing.
The Need is the internal, unconscious requirement — what they actually must confront to grow.
These must be in tension. A character who knows what they need is not interesting. The gap between Want and Need is where the story lives.

STRONG: A disgraced soldier hunting for the only witness who can prove a government cover-up (Want) who must confront that the system he served was always corrupt (Need)
WEAK: A detective who wants to solve a murder — no visible tension between external goal and internal wound

### 2. A Ticking Clock or Escalating Pressure
Something must be running out: time, resources, safety, trust, a window of opportunity.
Static pressure is not enough — it must escalate. Each delay should cost something.
The ticking clock creates urgency that forces the protagonist to act imperfectly under pressure.

STRONG: His node supply is running out — once it's gone, he loses his only edge and will be blamed for a massacre he didn't commit
WEAK: She needs to find answers before it's too late (vague — too late for what, exactly?)

### 3. A Unique World or Setting Detail That Feels Fresh
One specific detail that makes this story feel like it couldn't happen anywhere else.
Not a genre setting (dystopian city, magic academy) but a specific, unexpected angle on that setting.
The detail should create an immediate image that no other pitch in the genre produces.

STRONG: A metropolis carved into the walls of a mountain chasm, where magic runs on finite ammunition drained from living people
WEAK: A dark urban fantasy city with a hidden magical underworld

### 4. A Central Moral Dilemma or Impossible Choice
The protagonist must face a situation where every available option costs something real.
Not a puzzle with a clever solution — a genuine dilemma where the reader can't immediately see the right answer.
The dilemma should connect to the theme, not just the plot.

STRONG: Expose the conspiracy (and confirm his identity as the fugitive they're hunting) or disappear (and let the system keep feeding the poor into processing labs)
WEAK: She must choose between her career and her family (too abstract, no stakes specificity)

### 5. Hooks the Reader in the First Two Sentences
The logline and opening of the pitch must earn continued reading immediately.
No throat-clearing. No genre setup. Open on the specific situation, the wound, or the impossible position.
The reader should feel the pull before they've fully processed what they read.

STRONG: "Kor Morik just wanted to find Jace. For three years, he's been hunting through the vertical slums of Arch City, searching for the one surviving member of his black ops unit who can prove their government ordered them to massacre civilians."
WEAK: "In a world where magic and technology have merged, one former soldier will have to face his darkest hour..."

---

## ANTI-PATTERNS (automatic disqualify)

- The protagonist is reactive throughout — things happen to them rather than them driving events
- The stakes are vague ("save the world," "find the truth") without specificity
- The unique world element is a genre convention, not a fresh angle
- The moral dilemma resolves too easily in the reader's mind (one option is obviously right)
- The pitch summarizes plot rather than creating emotional pull
- Any AI-obvious phrasing in the logline or pitch text

---

## COMMERCIAL VIABILITY CHECKLIST

Beyond the five requirements, a strong pitch also:
- Fits recognizable genre expectations while adding a fresh angle (genre + twist)
- Has a protagonist the target reader will want to inhabit
- Has stakes that feel both personal (character) and large (world/society)
- Has a marketable, memorable logline (one sentence, twenty words or fewer)
- Avoids ideas that require extensive setup before the hook lands

---

## EVALUATION SCORING (when comparing multiple pitches)

Rank pitches on each criterion (1-5 scale), then weight:
- Hook strength in first two sentences: 25%
- Originality of world/setting detail: 20%
- Clarity and tension of moral dilemma: 20%
- Want/Need gap strength: 20%
- Ticking clock specificity: 15%

The highest-scoring pitch is rarely wrong. Trust the rubric over gut instinct when scores are close.
