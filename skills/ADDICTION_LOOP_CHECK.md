## Framework: The Four-Step Addiction Loop

Dopamine is the PREDICTION chemical, not the pleasure chemical. It fires during anticipation,
not reward. Addictive stories hack this by keeping an unresolved prediction running at all times.

### Step 1: STAKES
What activates the brain's prediction machine.
Requires three elements:
- A character the reader can root for (real human in real situation)
- Something at risk (concrete consequence if things go wrong)
- Urgency (ticking clock — compresses stakes and accelerates prediction)

Stakes DO NOT require life or death. They require personal relevance for the reader.
Stakes must be established in the first 200 words of any chapter.
Without stakes, no predictions fire. No predictions = no dopamine = no addiction.

"Vending machine" sign: Story starts by describing what happens. No character, no risk, no clock.

### Step 2: THE BIG QUESTION
The specific question that forces the reader to make a prediction.
Requirements:
- Must be explicit enough that the reader can actually form a guess
- Loaded early — the longer it sits unanswered, the longer the dopamine drip
- NOT vague teasers ("something crazy happened") — these don't trigger predictions
- Gives JUST enough info to guess, not so much it answers itself

The Big Question triggers a sustained low-grade dopamine drip (not a spike).
This is the "wanting to know" state. It must remain open long enough to create investment.

"Vending machine" sign: Reader is told what will happen rather than made to predict it.

### Step 3: THE HEAD FAKE
The outcome that breaks the prediction — but makes sense in retrospect.
Requirements:
- Contrasts what the reader was predicting based on established context
- MUST be logically consistent with prior clues (even if subtle)
- Reader should feel "I should have seen that" not "that came from nowhere"
- Size of dopamine spike is proportional to size of surprise

Critical distinction:
- BAD head fake: Random, no connection to setup. Reader feels cheated.
- GOOD head fake: Surprising but inevitable once revealed. Reader feels outsmarted.

Magician rule: Show them the hat, show it's empty, make them look away, THEN pull the rabbit.
Plant the clues. Let them form the wrong prediction. Subvert it logically.

"Vending machine" sign: The expected thing happens. No prediction is broken.

### Step 4: THE RE-HOOK
Opens the next loop as the previous loop closes. No gap allowed.
Timing is everything: the re-hook fires WHILE the head fake is still landing.
Requirements:
- A new question or complication that opens before the reader disengages
- Phrases that work: "which would have been great, except..." / "but here's the problem" /
  "and that's when I realized" / "which is exactly why..." / "have been perfect, if not for..."
- Never let a loop close without the next one already opening

The casino metric: decrease time between dopamine hits.
Dead air between loops = reader puts the book down.

"Vending machine" sign: Chapter ends with resolution and nothing new. Reader feels done.

## Evaluation Rubric

When checking a chapter for the addiction loop, score each element:

STAKES (0-3):
0 = No character/risk/urgency established
1 = Character present but risk or urgency missing
2 = All three present but weak or generic
3 = Character with specific stakes and ticking urgency — reader immediately cares

BIG QUESTION (0-3):
0 = No prediction loaded
1 = Vague tease but no specific question
2 = Question present but loaded too late or too obvious
3 = Specific, loaded early, creates multiple competing predictions

HEAD FAKE (0-3):
0 = Expected outcome. Prediction confirmed.
1 = Slight variation but largely predictable
2 = Genuine surprise but lacks logical retroactive consistency
3 = Breaks prediction AND makes sense — reader feels outsmarted, not cheated

RE-HOOK (0-3):
0 = Chapter ends. No new loop. Reader feels done.
1 = New question introduced but after a gap
2 = New loop opens at end of chapter
3 = New loop opens IN THE SAME BEAT as the head fake. No gap. Reader can't stop.

Total: 12 = Maximum addiction. 0-4 = Vending machine.

## Chapter Outline Structure (for Pipeline 2)

Every chapter outline should answer these four questions explicitly:

STAKES SETUP:
Who is the POV character and what do they want right now (external)?
What specific thing is at risk in THIS chapter (not the book — this chapter)?
What is the urgency/ticking clock in THIS chapter?

BIG QUESTION:
What question will the reader be asking by page 2 of this chapter?
What prediction will they be running?

HEAD FAKE:
What will the reader predict? What actually happens instead?
What clues in the setup make the real answer feel inevitable in retrospect?

RE-HOOK:
What is the final beat? What new question opens as this chapter ends?
Is it in the same sentence/breath as the chapter's resolution?

## First Draft Requirements

When writing a chapter, the following are REQUIRED (not optional):

OPENING (first 200 words):
- Establish the stakes trio: character + specific risk + urgency
- Do NOT open with backstory, description, or exposition before stakes
- Reader must know what they're predicting within 2 pages

EARLY MIDDLE:
- Load the Big Question explicitly — state or strongly imply it
- Give enough context for the reader to form competing predictions
- Do NOT resolve or answer the Big Question here

CHAPTER CLIMAX:
- Deliver the Head Fake — break the reader's prediction
- It must be logically consistent with prior clues
- Plant those clues during the Big Question setup

FINAL BEAT (last paragraph):
- Deliver the Re-hook — open the next loop before this one fully closes
- Use a complication, revelation, or new question
- The reader should feel physically unable to stop

## Red Flags to Flag in Prose

These patterns indicate a "vending machine" chapter:

- Chapter opens with description of setting before character stakes
- "Eventually," "soon," "later" — no urgency markers
- The reader learns what will happen before they can predict it
- Character does expected thing for expected reason with expected outcome
- Chapter ends on resolution with no new complication
- Transitions between scenes with no new question opened
- "And so they left" / "And that was that" — loop closers with no re-hook
- Surprise that has no retroactive logical basis
- Stakes that are abstract rather than specific and personal

## Re-hook Execution: Five Types

The Re-hook is the mechanism that opens the next loop. Five types, vary across chapters:

**1. Revelation Re-hook** — New information lands at the end that recontextualizes everything.
End on the image, before the reaction. The reaction becomes the engine of the next chapter.
STRONG: The last thing she sees is Daniel's badge — the one he reported stolen three months ago.
WEAK: She discovered Daniel was the mole. She didn't know what to do with that yet. (circuit partially closed)

**2. Action Re-hook** — Physical event cuts the chapter: gunshot, door closing, wire going dead.
Most overused type. Use sparingly — max 1 in 4 chapter endings. End on the specific image, not the label.
STRONG: The window shattered. She had time to see his face — just his face — before the lights went out.
WEAK: Suddenly, shots rang out! What would happen next?

**3. Decision Re-hook** — Protagonist commits to an irreversible choice. End before consequences arrive.
STRONG: She picked up the phone. One call would end this. She dialed.
WEAK: She wasn't sure what to do. She would have to decide soon.

**4. Question Re-hook** — New problem introduced just as another resolves. Reader exhales, then tenses.
STRONG: Everything made sense now — except one thing: why had they kept him alive for three days?
WEAK: She had a bad feeling she couldn't shake.

**5. Emotional Re-hook** — Relationship ruptured at maximum tension. No physical stakes required.
Most underused and often most powerful. Readers read all night for relationship resolution.
STRONG: He read the message twice. Then he put the phone face-down, poured the rest of his drink in the sink, and went to bed. He didn't reply.
WEAK: She was devastated by what he'd said.

**What kills the Re-hook:**
- Reaction Close: ending on the character's response to the event rather than the event itself
- Summary Close: narrator steps in and explains what the chapter meant
- False Alarm: chapter-ending stakes that deflate immediately at the start of the next chapter
- Dead air between loop close and next open — the Re-hook must fire IN THE SAME BEAT, not after a paragraph of resolution
