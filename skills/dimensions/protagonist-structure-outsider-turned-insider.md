## FILE ID
protagonist-structure-outsider-turned-insider

## DIMENSION TYPE
Protagonist Structure

## ONE-LINE DEFINITION
Never belonged, now getting access — the story is about what the protagonist discovers from inside and what that access costs them.

## FUNCTIONAL DEFINITION
Outsider-turned-insider protagonist structure places the protagonist in the position of gaining access to a world they were previously excluded from. Their advantage is the freshness of their perspective — they see what insiders cannot because they haven't been trained not to see it. Their disadvantage is that they lack the institutional knowledge, relationships, and standing that insiders take for granted.

The structure's central dynamic is the cost of access. The protagonist is admitted for a reason — someone wants them inside, or circumstances force them in — and that reason shapes what they discover and what they are permitted to see. Full insider access is typically withheld; they are insider enough to see things insiders see, but not insider enough to be safe.

## PROSE SIGNATURE
- The protagonist notices what insiders don't see because they don't know they're supposed to be blind to it.
- The cost of access is tracked throughout — what they must give up, perform, or become to maintain their position.
- Institutional culture is rendered through the outsider's learning curve — the reader learns the institution alongside the protagonist.
- Moments when the protagonist's outsider status is visible to insiders are specifically noted.

## CALIBRATION EXAMPLES
**Kazuo Ishiguro, Never Let Me Go** — Kathy gradually accesses the full truth of what Hailsham was and what she is. Her outsider-turned-insider arc is the novel's structure.

**Mohsin Hamid, The Reluctant Fundamentalist** — Changez gains access to American elite institutional culture and the novel traces what that access costs and reveals.

**NEAR-MISS — Tom Wolfe, The Bonfire of the Vanities** — Sherman McCoy is an insider who loses access rather than an outsider who gains it. The mistake is common: the structure is sometimes applied to protagonists who are actually insiders-turned-outsiders.

## FAILURE MODES
- The protagonist's outsider perspective is abandoned once they gain access — they become indistinguishable from insiders.
- Access is gained without cost — admission is free and comfortable.
- What the protagonist discovers from inside is available to any outside observer with sufficient research.

## AI WRITING DEFAULTS TO AVOID
The writing system will produce an outsider who adapts smoothly and completely → Smooth adaptation removes the outsider perspective that makes the structure valuable → The protagonist must retain their outsider perception even as they perform insider behavior.

The writing system will have the protagonist admitted without conditions → Conditional access is the structure's source of tension → Admission always comes with constraints — what the protagonist must be, do, or conceal to maintain their position.

## COMBINATION NOTES

- Combines with: plot-engine-revelation
- Emergent effect: The revelation the protagonist discovers from inside is specifically available only to insiders — their access was necessary to learn it, which makes the cost of gaining access and the value of the revelation proportional.

- Combines with: moral-register-grey
- Emergent effect: The inside reveals that the excluded position had legitimate grievances and the inside position has legitimate values — neither insider nor outsider perspective was simply right.
