## FILE ID
plot-engine-restoration

## DIMENSION TYPE
Plot Engine

## ONE-LINE DEFINITION
Something was broken, stolen, or lost — the story is the work of recovering or repairing it, and the protagonist's progress is measured against what was taken.

## FUNCTIONAL DEFINITION
Restoration plot engine organizes the story around a prior loss and the protagonist's effort to undo or repair it. The loss must be established before the story's central action begins: a family destroyed, a reputation ruined, a stolen object with specific significance, a community devastated. The protagonist's arc is measured against the restoration: how close are they to recovering what was lost? What must they sacrifice to get it back? Is full restoration possible, or only partial?

Restoration is distinct from survival: survival is about maintaining the current state against active threat. Restoration is about recovering a prior, better state that has been destroyed.

## READER PSYCHOLOGY
Restoration engine gives readers the experience of rooting for a specific, defined goal — the reader knows what success looks like from early in the story. This clarity of objective is the engine's primary offer: unlike revelation (where the goal is ambiguous) or information (where the goal is shifting), restoration tells the reader exactly what would constitute a happy ending.

What restoration withholds is ambiguity about the objective. Readers who find defined objectives limiting will experience restoration as mechanical.

## PROSE SIGNATURE
- The thing being restored is established with specific, sensory detail before it is lost or damaged — the reader must feel what was taken.
- Progress toward restoration is tracked explicitly — each scene advances or retreats the protagonist's position relative to the goal.
- The cost of restoration accumulates — what the protagonist sacrifices to restore what was lost must be tracked alongside what was lost.
- The restored state, when achieved, is compared to the original — restoration is never complete; something has been permanently changed.

## CALIBRATION EXAMPLES
**Kazuo Ishiguro, When We Were Orphans** — Christopher Banks's investigation is specifically about restoring his parents — the literal restoration of lost family. Ishiguro's prose tracks the protagonist's increasingly desperate calibration of what restoration would look like.

**Alexandre Dumas, The Count of Monte Cristo** — Restoration of honor, identity, and relationship drives every plot element. The engine runs on the specificity of what was taken and the methodical work of recovering it.

**NEAR-MISS — J.R.R. Tolkien, The Hobbit** — The restoration of Erebor is the nominal engine, but Bilbo's personal arc dominates the reader's engagement. The near-miss failure is that the restoration goal (the dwarves' mountain) is less emotionally established than the character arc, making the engine structural but not emotionally primary.

## FAILURE MODES
- What is to be restored is not established with enough specificity to produce genuine loss.
- Restoration is achieved too completely — if nothing has changed, the story denies the irreversibility of what happened.
- The cost of restoration is not tracked — the protagonist gets back what was taken without the proportional sacrifice that makes restoration meaningful.

## AI WRITING DEFAULTS TO AVOID
The writing system will restore what was lost completely → Complete restoration implies the loss didn't permanently change anything → Restoration must be partial or must change the protagonist in ways that prevent return to the prior state even after recovery.

The writing system will establish the loss through exposition → Exposition is information delivery, not felt loss → Establish what is to be restored through specific scenes that make the reader experience it before it is taken.

## CONFLICT WITH OTHER DIMENSIONS

- Conflicts with: moral-register-nihilistic
- What creates the friction: Restoration requires that a better prior state exists and can be approximated; nihilistic denies that better states are real moral categories.
- Default winner: moral-register-nihilistic — the engine requires moral categories that nihilistic denies.
- Override condition: plot-engine-restoration wins when the restoration is mechanistic rather than moral — something is technically recovered without the recovery having moral significance.

- Conflicts with: moral-register-tragic
- What creates the friction: Tragic requires that what is lost cannot be recovered; restoration requires the possibility of recovery.
- Default winner: moral-register-tragic — tragic structure denies what restoration requires.
- Override condition: plot-engine-restoration wins when the restoration attempt is itself the tragic mechanism — the effort to restore produces the destruction.

## COMBINATION NOTES

- Combines with: moral-register-clear-stakes
- Emergent effect: The restoration goal is morally legible — the reader understands exactly what is being recovered and why it matters, making each step toward or away from restoration a clear moral event.

- Combines with: protagonist-structure-reluctant-inheritor
- Emergent effect: The reluctant inheritor must restore something they didn't want to receive — they are doing the work of restoration for what they wish they hadn't been given, producing the specific experience of obligation without desire.
