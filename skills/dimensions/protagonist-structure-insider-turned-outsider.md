## FILE ID
protagonist-structure-insider-turned-outsider

## DIMENSION TYPE
Protagonist Structure

## ONE-LINE DEFINITION
Once belonged, now excluded — the protagonist knows how the system works from the inside and the story is about what they do with that knowledge from outside it.

## FUNCTIONAL DEFINITION
Insider-turned-outsider protagonist structure places the protagonist in a position of privileged knowledge without institutional access. They were trained by the system, formed by it, possibly defined by it — and then excluded. Their advantage is that they know exactly how it operates, who the real players are, what the actual rules are beneath the stated ones. Their disadvantage is that they have no formal standing to act on that knowledge.

This structure produces a specific form of tension: the protagonist's competence (insider knowledge) is in direct conflict with their position (exclusion from legitimate channels). Everything they do must be accomplished without the institutional resources they were trained to use.

The structure has a moral dimension: the insider-turned-outsider typically knows something the institution does not want known. Their exclusion is often the mechanism for silencing that knowledge, which makes their goal not just institutional return but the surfacing of what they know.

## READER PSYCHOLOGY
Insider-turned-outsider gives readers the experience of rooting for someone who has everything except standing. The reader can track both what the protagonist knows and why they can't use it through legitimate channels — this dual tracking is the structure's specific source of tension.

## PROSE SIGNATURE
- The protagonist's insider knowledge is demonstrated through specific institutional detail that outsiders would not have.
- Their exclusion is established through specific events, not general backstory.
- What they cannot do is as important as what they can — the constraints of their outsider position shape every choice.
- Other insiders react to them with the specific mixture of recognition and wariness they have for former members.

## CALIBRATION EXAMPLES
**John le Carré, The Spy Who Came in from the Cold** — Leamas's insider knowledge of the Circus is the engine of the plot; his exclusion from normal operational channels is the source of his vulnerability.

**Michael Clayton (film)** — Clayton's knowledge of Kenner, Bach, and Ledeen's wrongdoing comes from his insider position; his inability to act through legitimate channels is the story's tension.

**NEAR-MISS — Tom Clancy, The Hunt for Red October** — Ryan has insider knowledge of Soviet submarine technology but his outsider position is not a genuine constraint — institutional resources are made available to him. The near-miss failure is that the outsider position is established but not maintained as a constraint.

## FAILURE MODES
- The protagonist's outsider constraints are eliminated by a helper who provides institutional resources.
- The insider knowledge is generic rather than specific — what they know could be known by anyone who read the relevant books.
- Reintegration into the institution is the story's goal without adequate examination of what that costs.

## AI WRITING DEFAULTS TO AVOID
The writing system will produce institutional knowledge delivered through exposition → The insider's knowledge must be demonstrated through specific behavior and decision-making, not described → Show the insider-turned-outsider navigating situations in ways that only their specific training would enable.

The writing system will resolve the outsider constraint by giving the protagonist institutional resources → Providing resources removes the structural tension → The protagonist must accomplish their goal entirely outside legitimate institutional channels.
