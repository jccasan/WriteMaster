## FILE ID
plot-engine-escalation

## DIMENSION TYPE
Plot Engine

## ONE-LINE DEFINITION
Each event increases the stakes, scope, or threat level — the story cannot return to its starting conditions, and what is at risk grows with every plot beat.

## FUNCTIONAL DEFINITION
Escalation plot engine makes increasing stakes the story's structural principle. Each major plot event must expand either the threat (more powerful, more widespread, more personal), the scope (more people implicated, larger systemic consequences), or the cost (what the protagonist is willing to risk increases with each escalation). The story cannot return to its starting conditions — escalation is irreversible.

This is distinct from pacing-thriller-escalation, which governs chapter-level tension movement. Plot-engine-escalation governs the story's macro-structure: the story begins at a scale it cannot return to, and each act operates at a larger scale than the previous.

## READER PSYCHOLOGY
Escalation engine gives readers the experience of a story that keeps expanding its own terms — what was once the ceiling becomes the floor. This produces a specific kind of investment: the reader cannot imagine a resolution at the starting scale because the story has moved beyond it.

## PROSE SIGNATURE
- Each act's central conflict is larger in scope than the previous act's — not just more intense, but involving more people or higher-order systems.
- The protagonist's personal stakes increase proportionally — what they are willing to lose grows with each escalation.
- Reversals establish new floors rather than returning to prior positions — if the protagonist suffers a setback, they are set back to the current floor, not the original.

## CALIBRATION EXAMPLES
**Robert Harris, Fatherland** — The investigation begins as a murder and escalates to reveal a conspiracy that encompasses the foundations of the Reich. Each escalation opens a scope that makes the prior scope seem small.

**Gone Girl (film adaptation more than novel)** — The escalation engine is more visible in the film's structure: what begins as a missing persons case escalates to media circus to national obsession to mutual destruction.

**NEAR-MISS — James Patterson, Along Came a Spider** — Patterson attempts escalation but the scope shifts are not grounded in prior events — new escalations feel added rather than earned. The near-miss failure is that escalations arrive through author decision rather than through story logic, making them feel external to the narrative rather than inevitable.

## FAILURE MODES
- Escalations arrive through coincidence or authorial decision rather than story logic.
- The story returns to a prior scope after a setback, treating escalation as reversible.
- Personal stakes do not increase proportionally — the protagonist risks the same at the story's highest escalation as at its starting point.

## AI WRITING DEFAULTS TO AVOID
The writing system will produce escalations that increase intensity without increasing scope → Intensity without scope is thriller pacing, not escalation engine → Each escalation must implicate more people, more systems, or higher-order consequences than the previous level.

The writing system will allow recovery to prior scope → Recovery to prior scope denies escalation's irreversibility → Setbacks return the protagonist to the current floor, not to the starting conditions.

## CONFLICT WITH OTHER DIMENSIONS

- Conflicts with: moral-register-noir
- What creates the friction: Noir's premise is that the structure is corrupt from the start — there is no lower state to escalate from. Escalation implies a trajectory noir denies.
- Default winner: plot-engine-escalation — escalation can coexist with noir if what escalates is the protagonist's entanglement with the corrupt structure, not a movement from clean to corrupt.
- Override condition: moral-register-noir wins when the escalation reveals that the protagonist was already fully implicated from the start — the escalation is the revelation of prior corruption.

## COMBINATION NOTES

- Combines with: pacing-thriller-escalation
- Emergent effect: Plot scope and chapter tension both increase simultaneously — the story's subject (escalating stakes) and its structure (escalating chapter pressure) are identical, producing a story that cannot be experienced as anything other than relentless.

- Combines with: moral-register-tragic
- Emergent effect: Each escalation increases what the protagonist is required to sacrifice from their own moral integrity, until the final escalation requires them to sacrifice what made them who they are.

- Combines with: protagonist-structure-operative
- Emergent effect: Professional competence is systematically tested against escalating scope — the operative's skills were designed for a scale the story begins to exceed, making each escalation also a test of adaptation.
