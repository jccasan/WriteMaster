## FILE ID
moral-register-clear-stakes

## DIMENSION TYPE
Moral Register

## ONE-LINE DEFINITION
The story's moral landscape is legible — characters and readers share a common understanding of what is at risk and what choosing rightly or wrongly costs.

## FUNCTIONAL DEFINITION
Clear-stakes moral register does not mean simple morality or good-versus-evil plotting. It means the ethical architecture of the story is visible to both characters and readers simultaneously. When a character faces a choice, the reader understands what is at risk, what the character values, and what the cost of each path will be. The moral weight is legible without requiring narration to explain it.

This produces stories where choices matter in a specific, demonstrable way. The protagonist knows what they should do. They know what it will cost. The reader knows both. The drama is not in discovering what is right — it is in whether the protagonist can pay the price. This is what distinguishes clear-stakes from didactic fiction: the moral frame is shared, not instructed.

At the prose level, this requires that character motivation be transparent at the level of scene-level desire. Readers understand what each character wants and why. Actions have legible moral valence — not because the narrator assigns them, but because the story's architecture has established the terms clearly enough that behavior speaks for itself. Violence means something specific. Betrayal means something specific. The reader does not need to be told.

Clear-stakes does not promise that virtue is rewarded. It promises that virtue and vice are identifiable and that their costs are real.

## READER PSYCHOLOGY
Clear-stakes moral register gives readers the experience of being inside a story where their moral intuitions are valid and their engagement with character choice is meaningful. This is what adjacent registers like grey moral register specifically deny: readers in clear-stakes can root, judge, hope, and fear with confidence that the story is playing the same ethical game they are.

This is particularly effective in stories about sacrifice, duty, and the cost of integrity — readers need to believe that what is being sacrificed is real and that the sacrifice is genuinely a sacrifice, which requires shared moral legibility.

What clear-stakes withholds is moral complexity as an end in itself. Readers who want to inhabit irreducible ambiguity will find clear-stakes limiting. The register invites emotional investment, not philosophical destabilization.

## PROSE SIGNATURE
- Character choices are preceded by prose that makes the stakes of each option concrete and specific before the choice is made — the reader knows the terms before the decision lands.
- Motivation is transparent at the action level — a character's behavior reveals what they value without requiring interior monologue to explain it.
- Consequences track causally — when a character makes a choice, the consequences arrive through the choice's specific logic, not through coincidence or external pressure.
- Scene endings earn their emotional register — the final line can land on feeling because the reader has been given enough to warrant it.
- Dialogue is direct about desire, even when it is indirect about strategy — characters may conceal their plans but not their values.
- Betrayal, when it occurs, is rendered as a violation of something the story has clearly established as real — the reader feels the betrayal because the trust was legible.

## CALIBRATION EXAMPLES
**Ursula K. Le Guin, The Dispossessed** — The scene where Shevek decides to leave Anarres. The moral stakes are fully legible: what he will lose, what he hopes to gain, what his community will interpret as betrayal. Le Guin's prose does not need to explain the weight — it has built the architecture scene by scene so the reader supplies it.

**Hilary Mantel, Wolf Hall** — Throughout the trilogy, Cromwell's choices are morally legible even when morally uncomfortable. The reader always knows what Cromwell values, what he's trading away, and what he believes the trade is worth. This produces moral engagement rather than moral confusion.

**Kazuo Ishiguro, The Remains of the Day** — Stevens's choices are devastatingly legible in retrospect. The novel's architecture makes his moral failures visible to the reader before Stevens can admit them to himself. Clear-stakes does not require the character to have the reader's clarity — only that the reader has it.

**NEAR-MISS — Dan Brown, The Da Vinci Code** — The moral stakes are nominally clear but functionally absent — characters explain their stakes rather than demonstrating them through choice and consequence. The near-miss failure is that the protagonist's moral choices produce no real cost, making the legible stakes illusory: the reader knows what is supposedly at risk but never feels the risk.

## FAILURE MODES
- Stakes are stated by characters in dialogue rather than demonstrated through what they risk and lose.
- The protagonist faces a moral choice that has a clear right answer the reader can see from outside — genuine clear-stakes requires the right answer to come with real cost, not just recognition.
- Consequences fail to arrive from choice logic — the resolution depends on external providence rather than the moral architecture the story established.
- The story conflates moral legibility with moral simplicity — introducing only characters who are clearly good or clearly evil, rather than characters whose moral positions are clear but costly.
- The narrator editorializes on the moral weight of events rather than building the architecture that makes the weight self-evident.

## AI WRITING DEFAULTS TO AVOID
The writing system will produce characters who explain their moral stakes in dialogue → Explanation collapses clear-stakes into instruction; the reader needs to see the stakes built, not described → Replace explanatory dialogue with scenes that establish what characters have, what they value, and what they are risking through behavior and consequence.

The writing system will produce moral choices without real cost to the right decision → Clear-stakes without genuine cost is wish fulfillment, not moral architecture → Every right choice must cost something specific and irreversible; the reader should understand what the protagonist is paying.

The writing system will produce narration that assigns moral valence explicitly — "this was wrong," "she knew what she had to do" → Explicit narration replaces the architectural work that makes stakes legible → Build the frame so behavior speaks; cut the narrator's moral assignments.

## CONFLICT WITH OTHER DIMENSIONS

- Conflicts with: moral-register-noir
- What creates the friction: Noir insists ethical choices do not produce reliable outcomes; clear-stakes requires the moral architecture to be legible and consequential.
- Default winner: moral-register-noir — the moral architecture is more foundational; clear-stakes legibility cannot survive inside a system noir has defined as structurally corrupt.
- Override condition: Clear-stakes can operate inside a noir structure when confined to a character's private moral economy — what they owe to specific individuals — without claiming legibility at the systemic level.

- Conflicts with: moral-register-grey
- What creates the friction: Grey moral register insists reasonable people with good values genuinely disagree; clear-stakes assumes shared ethical legibility across character and reader.
- Default winner: Neither wins cleanly — the conflict is productive rather than resolved. The story must choose which register governs at the level of each specific choice: some choices can be grey while the stakes of making them are clear.
- Override condition: moral-register-clear-stakes wins when the story requires the reader to feel the cost of a character's choice; moral-register-grey wins when the story requires the reader to feel the genuine impossibility of a right answer.

- Conflicts with: moral-register-nihilistic
- What creates the friction: Nihilistic register denies that moral frameworks produce real outcomes; clear-stakes requires that the moral framework be both legible and consequential.
- Default winner: moral-register-nihilistic — a story cannot simultaneously present legible moral stakes and deny that ethical frameworks operate.
- Override condition: clear-stakes can surface as a character's earnest belief that the nihilistic structure then tests and dismantles — the stakes feel real to the character and reader until the register reveals they weren't.

- Conflicts with: pacing-slow-burn
- What creates the friction: Slow-burn pacing withholds information and defers resolution; clear-stakes requires the moral architecture to be built and legible, which requires the reader to have the relevant information.
- Default winner: pacing-slow-burn — pacing governs the rate of disclosure; the stakes can be clear when revealed without requiring early disclosure.
- Override condition: moral-register-clear-stakes wins when the stakes must be legible from early in the story to produce emotional investment in the choices that follow — the reader needs to care before the slow-burn can work.
