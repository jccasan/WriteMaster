LOGIC CHECK — INTERNAL CONSISTENCY AND PLAUSIBILITY AUDIT

Use this check on a story dossier or outline to identify logical inconsistencies, implausible plot elements, character-world mismatches, and world-building problems that undermine internal coherence. Output is a structured six-section audit report. Do NOT rewrite anything during the check — produce the report only.

---

## PRE-AUDIT PROCESS (internal — do not output)

Before producing the audit report, reason through these steps:
1. Identify the core premise and pitch
2. List all characters with their roles, goals, and abilities
3. Record all stated world-building rules and key details
4. Check each of the six audit categories systematically
5. Note every contradiction, implausibility, or logic gap
6. For each issue, think through what specifically breaks and how it could be resolved

---

## THE SIX AUDIT CATEGORIES

### 1. PREMISE LOGIC CHECK
- Does the core premise hold together internally?
- Are the central conflict and stakes consistent with the stated world rules?
- Does the "what if" setup make logical sense?
- If the premise requires the reader to accept something unusual, does the world's internal logic support it?

### 2. CHARACTER-WORLD FIT
- Do character roles, goals, and capabilities make sense within this world?
- Are any characters' abilities or positions inconsistent with the world's rules or social structures?
- Do relationships and motivations align with the premise and established tone?
- Could these specific characters exist in this specific world, or do they feel transplanted from a different genre or setting?

### 3. WORLD-BUILDING COHERENCE
- Do the world's elements support the premise without contradicting each other?
- Are there contradictions between different sections (magic rules vs. technology level, social structure vs. character freedom, etc.)?
- Do geography, technology/magic level, and social structures operate logically together?
- Are there world rules that are stated but then immediately violated?

### 4. PLOT SETUP PLAUSIBILITY
- Are there logistical impossibilities in the setup?
- Are there motivation gaps — reasons a character would obviously do something simple that would collapse the entire plot?
- Does the antagonist's power level make sense relative to the protagonist's resources?
- Is the inciting incident plausible given the established world and characters?
- Does the protagonist have access to what they need to attempt their goal, or are they missing something obvious?

### 5. EARLY-STAGE CONVENIENCE FLAGS
- Does the premise rely on unlikely coincidences or contrived setups?
- Do any characters exist only to solve plot problems (the mentor who appears exactly when needed, the ally who has exactly the right skill)?
- Are there perfect allies or resources that feel forced rather than earned?
- Are there "because the plot needs it" moments with no in-world logic?
- Does the antagonist have an obvious solution they're inexplicably not using?

### 6. SPECIFIC FIXES
For every major issue identified in sections 1–5, provide a fix block:

**Issue:** [Brief description of the logical flaw]
**Why it breaks:** [Explanation of the inconsistency or implausibility]
**Suggested fix:** [Specific, actionable recommendation that preserves the core appeal of the story]

If no significant issues were identified in sections 1–5:
"No significant issues identified. The dossier is internally consistent."

---

## OUTPUT FORMAT

Produce the complete six-section audit report. For each section:
- If issues exist: describe each one specifically, with enough detail to act on
- If no issues: "No significant issues identified in this category."

Section 6 must include a fix block for EVERY major issue from sections 1–5. Minor stylistic or naming issues do not require fix blocks — only logical, structural, and plausibility failures.

---

## SEVERITY TIERS

When writing Section 6, prioritize fixes in order:

**Critical (must fix)**: Issues that prevent the story from working at all — premise that contradicts itself, protagonist motivation that collapses under mild scrutiny, world rules that are immediately violated

**Significant (should fix)**: Issues that a reader would notice and that would pull them out of the story — convenient rescues, character abilities that appear without establishment, antagonist who could easily win but doesn't

**Minor (consider fixing)**: Issues that careful readers might note but that don't break the story — small timeline implausibilities, character positions that require some reader good faith

Label each fix block with its severity tier.

---

## WHAT THIS CHECK IS NOT

- Not a prose quality check — that's the AI-isms or style check
- Not a theme/emotional payoff check — that's the emotional check
- Not a continuity check across chapters — that's the chronology check
- Not a character name check — that's the character name check

This check is exclusively about: does this story make sense as a logical system? Could it exist as described?
