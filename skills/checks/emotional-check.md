EMOTIONAL CHECK — THEME COHESION AND EMOTIONAL PAYOFF AUDIT

Use this check on a story dossier, outline, or completed draft to evaluate whether the emotional and thematic architecture is working. Output is an improvement plan — a numbered list of specific, actionable fixes. Do NOT rewrite anything during the check — produce the plan only.

---

## THE FIVE AUDIT CATEGORIES

### 1. THEME COHESION

Theme is a moral argument tested through the plot — not a label, not a topic.
"Love" is not a theme. "Love that demands sacrifice eventually demands everything" is a theme.

For each stated theme in the document:
- Is it expressed as a moral argument (X leads to Y, the cost of Z is W)?
- Is it tested through specific plot events that force the protagonist to choose?
- List every major story moment where the theme should be present but currently feels absent or weak
- List every moment where the theme is told (a character states it) rather than shown (a choice demonstrates it)

A theme that has no cost — where the protagonist can hold their correct belief without paying for it — is decorative, not structural.

### 2. EMOTIONAL PAYOFF

The protagonist's arc must deliver a genuine gut punch. Check the lie-to-truth sequence:

- **The Lie**: Is the protagonist's flawed belief (Ghost → Lie) concrete and specific? Does it have a traceable origin event?
- **The Cost**: Does the protagonist pay a real price for holding the Lie before the story forces them to change? (Not consequences imposed externally, but ones caused by their own blind spot)
- **The Midpoint**: Is there a genuine shift from reaction to action? The protagonist should stop running and start fighting (or vice versa) at a specific, identifiable moment
- **The Climax**: Does the protagonist face a choice that can only be resolved by accepting the Truth? Can they solve the external problem without confronting the internal one? If yes, the arc isn't structurally bound to the plot — fix it
- **The Payoff**: Does the closing image mirror the opening in a way that shows change? What specifically has the protagonist lost, gained, or become?

Flag every place the arc feels arbitrary rather than earned.

### 3. CHARACTER MOTIVATION

For each named character, check:
- Is their Want (conscious goal) in tension with their Need (what they actually require to grow)?
- Is their motivation specific to their history, role, and world — or generic (greed, power, revenge)?
- Does the antagonist mirror or invert the protagonist's Lie? (The antagonist is who the protagonist becomes if they never change)
- Do supporting characters have their own agendas that operate independently of whether the protagonist needs them?
- Are there characters who exist only to deliver information, open doors, or save the protagonist? Flag these

### 4. SUSPENSE ENGINE

- Is there a ticking clock or escalating deadline? Is it specific (runs out in X chapters/days) or vague ("time is running out")?
- Does each major scene or chapter end with an open circuit — an unresolved question, a new complication, or a decision pending?
- Is the Zeigarnik effect operating? Would a reader have an unfinished feeling that pulls them forward?
- Is the pressure escalating across the three acts, or does it plateau after the midpoint?

### 5. CONVENIENCE AUDIT

This is the most common structural failure in AI-assisted story development. Flag any:
- Coincidences that give the protagonist what they need without them earning it
- Convenient discoveries that appear exactly when the plot requires them
- External rescues (someone saves the protagonist at a critical moment when they should have to save themselves)
- Characters who appear only when the plot needs them and disappear otherwise
- Information that reaches the protagonist through no logical mechanism
- The antagonist making a critical mistake specifically so the protagonist can win

---

## OUTPUT FORMAT

Produce the output as:

**EMOTIONAL IMPROVEMENT PLAN**

Number each item. For each issue:
- State which category it falls under (Theme / Emotional Payoff / Character Motivation / Suspense / Convenience)
- Describe the specific problem in one to two sentences
- Provide a specific, actionable fix — not "strengthen the theme" but "add a scene at the midpoint where [character] must choose between [option A] and [option B], both of which cost them something related to [Lie]"

If a category has no issues, write: "No issues found in [Category]."

---

## QUALITY STANDARD FOR THIS CHECK

A good emotional improvement plan is:
- Specific to THIS story, not generic craft advice
- Actionable — the author knows exactly what to add, change, or cut
- Prioritized — the most structural failures (arc not bound to plot, missing ticking clock) come before stylistic ones
- Honest — if the protagonist's arc is cosmetic rather than structural, say so directly
