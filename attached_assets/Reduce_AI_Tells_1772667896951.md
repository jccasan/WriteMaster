## Executive summary

This document focuses on **legitimate quality improvements**: making model outputs read as **more natural, specific, and voice-consistent** to humans, while reducing patterns many readers perceive as ÔÇťAI-ish.ÔÇŁ It does **not** recommend deceptive practices (for example, misrepresenting authorship in violation of policies, contracts, or academic rules). Across the literature and official guidance, the biggest quality drivers cluster into four levers:

2. **Decoding controls** (temperature/top-p/top-k, nucleus sampling). Nucleus sampling was introduced specifically to reduce ÔÇťdegenerateÔÇŁ generation while preserving diversity. ţłÇciteţłéturn0search0ţłéturn0search11ţłéturn10search13ţłü

4. **Training-time adaptation** when you need consistent voice at scale (SFT; preference tuning like DPO; parameter-efficient tuning like LoRA/QLoRA). ## Background on common AI ÔÇťtellsÔÇŁ and human writing markers

### What people perceive as ÔÇťAI-ishÔÇŁ patterns

Research on open-ended generation repeatedly documents a cluster of artifacts that correlate with low-quality or machine-like text, especially under certain decoding strategies:

**Over-smoothness and genericness.** Models often default to safe, high-probability phrasing, producing ÔÇťblandÔÇŁ continuations; this is closely tied to decoding/training dynamics and is discussed in work on degeneration and dullness. ţłÇciteţłéturn0search0ţłéturn12search0ţłü

**Repetition loops.** Both decoding-time effects and training objectives can yield repetitive phrases, sentence structures, and topic cycling. Unlikelihood training was proposed specifically because standard likelihood training can assign too much probability to repetitive sequences. ţłÇciteţłéturn12search0ţłü

**Template rhetoric.** Readers frequently flag patterns like: over-signposting (ÔÇťIn conclusionÔÇŽÔÇŁ), symmetrical bulleting, and ÔÇťbalanced but emptyÔÇŁ disclaimers. Human raters in experiments also associate ÔÇťnonsensicalÔÇŁ and ÔÇťrepetitiveÔÇŁ features with AI outputs, even when they fail to reliably classify origin overall. ţłÇciteţłéturn16view0ţłéturn14search1ţłü

**Distributional oddities under naive sampling.** The nucleus sampling line of work frames an ÔÇťunreliable tailÔÇŁ problem: pure sampling can produce incoherent or degenerate text, motivating truncation-based approaches like top-p. ţłÇciteţłéturn0search4ţłéturn0search12ţłü

**Stylometric regularities.** Multiple studies show that **stylometric features** (function words, POS patterns, punctuation distributions, etc.) can separate many LLM outputs from human writing in specific settings, even if humans themselves struggle. ţłÇciteţłéturn14search16ţłéturn14search0ţłéturn16view0ţłü

### Markers of human writing that models often underproduce by default

Human writing markers are not magic ÔÇťtells.ÔÇŁ They are tendencies that show up in stylometry and in human-evaluation frameworks: variation, coherence, relevance, and contextual appropriateness. ţłÇciteţłéturn14search25ţłéturn14search1ţłü

Common markers that increase perceived humanity (especially to readers, not detectors):

**Concrete situated detail.** Humans naturally anchor text in specific constraints (time, place, stakes, and trade-offs).  

**Uneven rhythm and intentional emphasis.** Sentence length and structure vary, sometimes for rhetorical effect, sometimes because humans revise locally. Stylometric analyses often leverage these variations. ţłÇciteţłéturn14search16ţłéturn14search0ţłü  

**Authorial intent and selective omission.** Humans choose what *not* to say; models often over-explain unless constrained. Prompt-level instruction and evaluation rubrics can directly push toward concision where appropriate. ţłÇciteţłéturn10search1ţłéturn14search1ţłü  

**Revision fingerprints.** Human text often reads like it has been edited: tightened openings, cut redundancies, clarified nouns, improved verb choices. Iterative refinement methods operationalize this idea for LLM outputs. ţłÇciteţłéturn17search0ţłü

## Evidence-based techniques to improve creativity and human realism

This section maps the requested techniques to what research and official docs imply works reliably in practice.

### Prompt engineering and few-shot exemplars

**Why it works:** large models can learn task behavior from a handful of examples (ÔÇťin-context learningÔÇŁ), demonstrated prominently in the GPT-3 few-shot learning results. ţłÇciteţłéturn3search0ţłéturn3search8ţłü

**What consistently improves humanlike writing quality:**
- **Explicit ÔÇťvoice contractÔÇŁ** (tone, persona boundaries, taboo phrases, pacing) placed in the highest-priority instruction channel available (for example, system/developer instructions depending on API). OpenAIÔÇÖs prompt engineering guidance emphasizes instruction priority and using high-level instructions to control behavior. ţłÇciteţłéturn10search1ţłéturn14search3ţłü  
- **Few-shot ÔÇťstyle anchorsÔÇŁ**: 2ÔÇô6 short exemplars paired with brief rationales (ÔÇťwhy this is goodÔÇŁ) often yields better style adherence than abstract descriptors alone. This aligns with guidance to provide examples of good output. ţłÇciteţłéturn10search18ţłéturn5search11ţłü  
- **Negative constraints** (ÔÇťavoid these phrases,ÔÇŁ ÔÇťdo not use listicles,ÔÇŁ ÔÇťno corporate fillerÔÇŁ) combined with positive targets (ÔÇťsound like X: crisp, opinionated, specificÔÇŁ).  

[Inference] If your goal is ÔÇťmore human to readers,ÔÇŁ a strong heuristic is to anchor the output in **specific context** (stakes, constraints, sensory detail, trade-offs). This pushes the model away from ÔÇťgeneric completionÔÇŁ mode and toward ÔÇťsituated drafting.ÔÇŁ

### System messages and instruction hierarchy

Modern production systems often distinguish instruction roles (system vs developer vs user). OpenAI documentation explicitly notes that instructions can have different authority levels and higher-level instructions take priority over input prompts. ţłÇciteţłéturn10search1ţłéturn14search3ţłü

Implication for writing quality: put your **stable voice rules and taboo patterns** in the highest authority channel, and keep user prompts mostly factual and context-heavy.

### Temperature, top-p, top-k, and decoding strategy

**Core findings:**
- Nucleus (top-p) sampling was introduced to mitigate generation degeneration and improve overall generation quality under human evaluation compared to other decoding strategies. ţłÇciteţłéturn0search0ţłéturn0search12ţłéturn0search8ţłü  
- OpenAIÔÇÖs API reference describes `top_p` as nucleus sampling and notes you generally adjust `temperature` or `top_p`, but not both. ţłÇciteţłéturn0search11ţłéturn5search13ţłü  
- In open-source `transformers`, generation parameters have documented defaults (for example, `top_k` default 50, `top_p` default 1.0, temperature default 1.0 when not set). ţłÇciteţłéturn10search13ţłéturn3search27ţłü  
- Greedy decoding is a baseline that can become repetitive for longer outputs; Hugging Face guidance flags this explicitly. ţłÇciteţłéturn10search24ţłü  

**Practical interpretation:**
- For **creative writing**, you generally want *some* stochasticity (sampling) plus controls against tail weirdness (top-p and/or top-k).  
- For **brand writing**, you often want moderate creativity but very consistent tone; this favors **moderate temperature** plus **reranking** (see below) instead of extreme sampling settings.  

### Chain-of-thought control and reasoning visibility

Chain-of-thought prompting can improve reasoning performance on complex tasks. ţłÇciteţłéturn0search1ţłéturn0search17ţłü  
Self-consistency (sampling multiple reasoning paths and selecting the most consistent answer) improves performance further in reasoning benchmarks. ţłÇciteţłéturn3search2ţłü

For **creative writing**, the practical use is usually not ÔÇťshow your chain-of-thought,ÔÇŁ but:
- Ask the model for a **short outline/beat sheet** first (or generate it internally), then write the final prose.
- Keep the final output free of meta-reasoning unless the application wants it.

### Style transfer and controllable generation

Control can be introduced either at inference time or via training:

- **Control codes**: CTRL trains on control codes to govern style/content/task behavior. ţłÇciteţłéturn4search1ţłéturn4search9ţłü  
- **Plug-and-play control**: PPLM steers generation using lightweight attribute models without retraining the base LM, enabling topic/sentiment steering. ţłÇciteţłéturn4search0ţłéturn4search32ţłü  
- **Classic neural style transfer**: cross-alignment methods do style transfer from non-parallel text. This is useful background, though modern LLM practice more often uses instruction conditioning or fine-tuning instead. ţłÇciteţłéturn4search2ţłéturn4search18ţłü  

### Fine-tuning, RLHF, DPO, and data augmentation

**Training-time methods** are most justified when you need high volume and consistent voice, or when prompt-only steering is too expensive in tokens/latency.

- **RLHF-style alignment** (InstructGPT) shows that fine-tuning with human feedback can produce models preferred by humans over much larger base models. ţłÇciteţłéturn0search2ţłéturn0search22ţłü  
- **DPO** is a simpler preference-optimization approach that avoids explicit reward modeling and RL loops; it is widely used as an RLHF alternative. ţłÇciteţłéturn2search2ţłéturn5search19ţłü  
- **LoRA** reduces the number of trainable parameters by injecting low-rank adapters while freezing base weights. ţłÇciteţłéturn2search0ţłéturn15search25ţłü  
- **QLoRA** extends this with 4-bit quantization to enable fine-tuning very large models with significantly lower memory. ţłÇciteţłéturn2search1ţłü  
- **OpenAI SFT guidance** and best practices emphasize embedding the instructions/prompts that worked pre-fine-tune into every training example for generalization. ţłÇciteţłéturn5search11ţłéturn5search22ţłü  

Data augmentation (for training style/voice or robustness):
- Back-translation is a classic method for leveraging monolingual data (originating in NMT literature). ţłÇciteţłéturn13search4ţłéturn13search0ţłü  
- Simple text augmentation operations (EDA) can help in small-data settings. ţłÇciteţłéturn13search1ţłéturn13search5ţłü  
- Surveys caution that augmentation impacts meaning vs form and should be used intentionally. ţłÇciteţłéturn13search30ţłü  

### Post-processing heuristics

There is a clear research distinction between:
- **Training/decoding fixes** for repetition and degeneration (nucleus sampling, unlikelihood training). ţłÇciteţłéturn0search0ţłéturn12search0ţłü  
- **Editorial post-processing** for style consistency and readability (linting, rewriting, human review), which is best seen as a product workflow aligned with human evaluation best practices. ţłÇciteţłéturn14search1ţłéturn17search0ţłü  

[Inference] Post-processing is most valuable when it is framed as **quality control** (remove redundancy, enforce house style, ensure the piece contains concrete details) rather than as ÔÇťconcealment.ÔÇŁ

### Prompt template: voice contract + constraints

Below is a general template you can adapt across providers. (Names like ÔÇťsystemÔÇŁ/ÔÇťdeveloperÔÇŁ vary by API.)

```text

- Tone: confident, practical, occasionally opinionated.
- Rhythm: varied sentence length; avoid repetitive openers.
- Specificity: include concrete details (numbers, scenarios, constraints) when available.
- Avoid: generic filler (e.g., "in today's world", "it's important to note", "in conclusion"),

- Format: [Markdown | plain text | JSON field final_text] (UNSPECIFIED by user; choose per app)

- Must respect: safety policies, privacy, and any provided brand rules.

1) Draft.
2) Self-edit once: remove filler, increase concreteness, tighten verbs.
```

### Best-of-N sampling and reranking recipe

Research and practice increasingly rely on ÔÇťgenerate multiple, then pick bestÔÇŁ (best-of-N / reranking). ţłÇciteţłéturn17search6ţłéturn17search2ţłü

A lightweight implementation (model-agnostic):
1. Generate N candidates with moderate creativity.
2. Score each candidate with a rubric.
3. Select top-1 (or top-2 for optional human choice).

**Rubric dimensions (recommended):**
- Human voice match
- Specificity and concreteness
- Coherence and flow
- Non-repetition
- Factuality and appropriate uncertainty (if facts are involved)

This connects directly to human evaluation criteria like fluency, coherence, relevance, and factuality. ţłÇciteţłéturn14search5ţłéturn14search25ţłü

### Iterative self-refinement recipe

Self-Refine formalizes a loop where the model critiques a draft and then revises it, improving human preference outcomes without training. ţłÇciteţłéturn17search0ţłéturn17search12ţłü

```text

Step 2: Feedback (critic)
- List the 3 biggest "robotic" patterns: filler, repetition, generic claims
- List 3 concrete details that could be added if available

- Tighten verbs

```

### Post-processing filters: a ÔÇťstyle lintÔÇŁ pass

A style-lint pass can catch visible ÔÇťbottyÔÇŁ artifacts before publishing. This is best treated as editorial QA, not as an attempt to bypass provenance systems. ţłÇciteţłéturn14search1ţłéturn1search3ţłü

```python
import re
from collections import Counter

BANNED_PHRASES = [
    r"\bas an ai language model\b",
    r"\bit is important to note\b",
    r"\bin conclusion\b",
    r"\btoday['ÔÇÖ]s (fast[- ]paced )?world\b",
]

def sentence_starts(text: str, n: int = 12):
    # naive sentence split; replace with spaCy or nltk in production
    sents = re.split(r"(?<=[.!?])\s+", text.strip())
    starts = []
    for s in sents[:n]:
        words = re.findall(r"[A-Za-z']+", s.lower())
        if words:
            starts.append(words[0])
    return starts

def lint_human_voice(text: str) -> dict:
    flags = []

    lowered = text.lower()
    for pat in BANNED_PHRASES:
        if re.search(pat, lowered):
            flags.append(f"contains_banned_phrase:{pat}")

    starts = sentence_starts(text)
    if starts:
        most_common, count = Counter(starts).most_common(1)[0]
        if count >= 3:
            flags.append(f"repetitive_sentence_openers:{most_common}:{count}")

    # simple repetition heuristic: repeated trigrams
    tokens = re.findall(r"[A-Za-z']+", lowered)
    trigrams = zip(tokens, tokens[1:], tokens[2:])
    tri_counts = Counter(trigrams)
    rep_tris = [tri for tri, c in tri_counts.items() if c >= 2]
    if rep_tris:
        flags.append(f"repeated_trigrams:{len(rep_tris)}")

    return {"flags": flags}

# Example usage:
# result = lint_human_voice(draft_text)
# if result["flags"]: send back for revision with flags
```

## Evaluation metrics and human-in-the-loop testing protocols

### Automatic metrics that correlate with ÔÇťhumanlikeÔÇŁ generation properties

No single metric fully captures creativity or human realism, so use a **bundle**:

- **MAUVE**: measures divergence between model text and human text distributions and reports correlation with human judgments in open-ended tasks in its original work. ţłÇciteţłéturn11search0ţłéturn11search32ţłü  
- **Diversity metrics**: Distinct-n is widely used but has known issues; Self-BLEU measures diversity by comparing generated samples to each other. ţłÇciteţłéturn12search2ţłéturn12search23ţłéturn12search7ţłü  
- **Repetition measures**: duplicate n-grams and repetition rates are commonly tracked; repetition is a known failure mode. ţłÇciteţłéturn12search0ţłéturn10search24ţłü  
- **Semantic similarity metrics** (when you have references): BERTScore and BLEURT better capture paraphrase/semantic similarity than pure n-gram overlap in many settings. ţłÇciteţłéturn11search1ţłéturn11search10ţłéturn11search6ţłü  
- **Human-vs-machine discrimination frameworks**: HUSE combines human and statistical evaluation and highlights that optimizing ÔÇťqualityÔÇŁ can reduce diversity. ţłÇciteţłéturn11search7ţłéturn11search15ţłü  

Practical guidance: use automatic metrics primarily for **regression testing** (detecting changes across model versions, prompts, or decoding settings), not as final truth. Human evaluation best-practice surveys emphasize variability and pitfalls in evaluation design. ţłÇciteţłéturn14search1ţłéturn14search13ţłü

### Human evaluation design that actually works

A best-practice review of human evaluation in NLG emphasizes standardizing tasks, instructions, and reporting to reduce inconsistency. ţłÇciteţłéturn14search1ţłéturn14search29ţłü

Recommended protocol (application-ready):

**Define evaluation axes (pick 3ÔÇô6):**
- Fluency
- Coherence
- Relevance/usefulness
- Factuality (if applicable)
- Voice match / persona fit
- Originality (task-dependent) ţłÇciteţłéturn14search5ţłéturn14search25ţłü  

**Prefer pairwise comparisons over absolute scoring** when feasible. Pairwise setups can be more stable and can be made more annotation-efficient with active selection methods. ţłÇciteţłéturn14search2ţłéturn14search6ţłü  

**Measure inter-rater agreement** and adjudicate disagreements for high-stakes evaluation. Best-practice frameworks emphasize careful planning and review phases. ţłÇciteţłéturn14search1ţłéturn16view1ţłü  

### LLM-as-a-judge: when to use it and how to control risk

LLM-as-a-judge has become a scalable way to approximate human preferences in open-ended generation, but it carries biases (position bias, verbosity bias, self-enhancement) and needs calibration. ţłÇciteţłéturn17search21ţłéturn17search29ţłéturn17search37ţłü

A safe, robust setup:
