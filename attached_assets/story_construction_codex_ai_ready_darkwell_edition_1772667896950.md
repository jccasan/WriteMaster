# Story Construction Codex (AI-Ready) — Darkwell Edition

*A comprehensive, instruction-first codex for outlining, drafting, and revising novels with AI while preserving human intent, continuity, and craft.*

---

## 0) Operating Principles (The Contract)

### 0.1 The story is a contract
- The reader pays attention in exchange for **empathy** (connection) and **curiosity** (tension). Every scene must serve at least one of these drives.
- If a passage is “pretty” but does not increase empathy or curiosity, it is suspect.

### 0.2 Suspense is the universal engine
- Suspense is **delayed closure**. It works in any genre.
- Maintain an active question: *what remains unknown, unresolved, or at risk?*

### 0.3 Open circuits win
- The brain remembers the unfinished. End chapters with an **open circuit** (a question, a new risk, a reveal that demands a response).
- Avoid full closure unless you are ending a major act or the book.

### 0.4 Authenticity over perfection
- Readers forgive imperfections. They do not forgive fake emotion.
- Behavior, tone, and consequence must match the character’s inner truth.

---

## 1) Macro-to-Micro Workflow (How to Build Without Getting Lost)

### 1.1 Zoom levels
A novel is architecture:
- **Macro (Blueprint):** premise, theme, plot/structure, arcs, world rules, POV strategy.
- **Micro (Bricks):** scenes, beats, dialogue, pacing, prose, sensory grounding.

Write and revise in **macro → micro** order. Do not line-edit a story that still has structural cracks.

### 1.2 Iteration rule
The process is not linear. It is cyclical:
1. Decide the next macro constraint.
2. Draft the next micro unit that proves it.
3. Re-evaluate macro implications.
4. Repeat.

---

## 2) Premise, Theme, and Character Transformation

### 2.1 Premise (one sentence)
Use this formula:
> **An (evocative protagonist), in (a destabilizing situation), must (do X) or else (stakes Z).**

Premise must include:
- Protagonist
- Objective
- Antagonistic force/obstacle
- Stakes (what breaks if they fail)

### 2.2 Theme as a question
Theme is not a slogan. It is an argument your plot dramatizes.
- Start with the premise.
- Ask: *What human problem does this pressure-test?*

### 2.3 Lie vs Truth (engine of arc)
- **Lie:** the protagonist’s functional misconception.
- **Truth:** the thematic principle the story forces them to accept.

The plot is a crucible designed to make the Lie fail.

### 2.4 Anti-theme (make the conflict real)
The Lie must have real benefits. If the Lie is never useful, the central dilemma has no weight.

---

## 3) Structure Options (Choose the Tool That Fits the Story)

### 3.1 Three-Act (general-purpose)
- Act I: setup → disruption → point of no return.
- Act II: escalating conflict → midpoint shift → deeper cost.
- Act III: final confrontation → resolution → denouement.

### 3.2 Seven-Point Structure (Dan Wells)
A symmetrical, character-change-first scaffold. Plan it **backwards**.

**The Seven Points (in planning order):**
1. **Resolution** (final state of protagonist)
2. **Hook** (starting state, mirrored opposite)
3. **Midpoint** (shift from reaction to action)
4. **Plot Turn 1** (inciting incident / threshold)
5. **Plot Turn 2** (aha tool/insight that enables victory)
6. **Pinch Point 1** (antagonist flexes; stakes become concrete)
7. **Pinch Point 2** (all is lost; plan breaks; forces true solution)

Use it when you want:
- a clean arc
- an active antagonist
- a midpoint that truly changes the story’s posture

### 3.3 Fichtean Curve (crisis escalation)
A relentless pressure engine, common in thrillers/mysteries.

**Shape:**
- Long rising action (about two-thirds): **jagged, escalating crises**
- Late climax
- Brief falling action

Rule of escalation:
- Each attempted solution causes the next, worse crisis.
- Allow small “breathing dips” without restoring safety.

Use it when you want:
- page-turn momentum
- minimal setup
- constant re-framing via new crises/revelations

### 3.4 Hybrid guidance
It is normal to blend.
- Use **Seven-Point** for the arc spine.
- Use **Fichtean crisis peaks** to shape the middle.

---

## 4) Plot Pressure and Time Discipline

### 4.1 The ticking element
Every novel needs an approaching event/decision that anchors anticipation:
- trial, gala, storm, election, deadline, countdown, expiring leverage

### 4.2 Cohesion and continuity
The book must feel like one continuous moral and emotional argument.

### 4.3 Economy of convenience
Avoid coincidences and unearned discoveries.
- A reveal should be earned through action or consequence.

### 4.4 Time discipline
Orient early. Maintain consistent temporal signals.
- If time is fuzzy, cause-and-effect collapses.

### 4.5 Opening architecture (first ten pages)
Deliver:
- conflict
- a character worth following
- forward motion

---

## 5) Scene Engineering (The Atomic Unit)

### 5.1 Scene definition
A scene is a continuous unit of time and space from a single POV.

### 5.2 The Scene Triad
Every scene must have:
1. **Goal:** what the POV character wants *now* (tangible).
2. **Conflict:** what blocks it (external or internal).
3. **Outcome / Value Shift:** how the situation changes by the end.

If nothing changes, the scene does not earn the page.

### 5.3 Begin late, end early
- Enter at the moment of pressure.
- Exit right after the turn.

### 5.4 Double-up rule
No scene should do only one job.
Aim for at least two:
- plot + character
- information + conflict
- action + intimacy
- world detail + consequence

### 5.5 Engines you can plug into any scene
- concealed agenda
- lie
- threat
- moral dilemma
- ticking clock
- asymmetry of information (reader knows something a character doesn’t)

---

## 6) Chapters and Pacing

### 6.1 Chapters are reader experience controls
- Short chapters accelerate urgency.
- Longer chapters can deepen interiority or atmosphere.

### 6.2 End on an open circuit
- A new question.
- A reveal that changes the plan.
- A cost that forces a choice.

---

## 7) Dialogue, Prose, and “Mundane Friction”

### 7.1 Dialogue refinement rules
- Read dialogue aloud.
- Cut filler unless it carries subtext.
- Give each speaker a distinct default rhythm and vocabulary.

### 7.2 Show vs tell (use “tell” strategically)
- Transform bland telling into action and sensory proof.
- Keep exposition embedded inside conflict.

### 7.3 Mundane friction (ground the scene)
In each scene, add 1–2 ordinary physical frictions that affect behavior:
- AC whine, bad Wi‑Fi, sweat under collar, sticky controls, flickering light

These details should create constraints or choices, not just mood.

---

## 8) World-Building as a Story Function

### 8.1 Two build approaches
- **Inside-out:** build only what the plot needs.
- **Outside-in:** build a deep world, then choose the story inside it.

### 8.2 Pillars to consider
- physicality (geography, climate)
- culture/society
- history/politics
- systems (magic/technology), with consistent costs and limits

### 8.3 Iceberg principle
Show the top 10% on-page. The rest supports plausibility.

---

## 9) AI Collaboration Protocol (How to Prompt Without Slop)

### 9.1 The FITS prompt pattern
Use:
- **Framework:** structure, format, constraints
- **Identity:** role the model should inhabit (editor, thriller architect)
- **Task:** what to produce, exactly
- **Style:** voice, tense, POV, heat level, taboo words, etc.

### 9.2 Specific beats vague
AI cannot infer your intent like a human collaborator.
- If you want a thing, ask for it.

### 9.3 Less is more
- Overstuffing prompts waters down priorities.
- Prefer summaries, dossiers, and chapter recap bullets instead of dumping full manuscripts.

### 9.4 Use containers
For large prompts, segment with clear headers or XML-style tags so the model doesn’t lose the needle.

### 9.5 The Fractal Technique (small → big)
- brainstorm → pitch → synopsis → outline → scene list → draft

### 9.6 “Checks” loop (quality control)
After generating a story dossier or outline, run:
- **Emotional check:** theme cohesion, payoff strength, character resonance
- **Logic check:** causal chain, plausibility, timeline, tradecraft realism
- **Name check:** obvious AI-name artifacts
Then rewrite the dossier implementing only improvements.

---

## 10) The Story Dossier Template (AI-Friendly)

Use this as your canonical pre-draft doc.

### 10.1 Dossier fields
1. **Premise (1–2 sentences)**
2. **Theme question**
3. **Protagonist**
   - external goal (Want)
   - internal need (Truth)
   - Lie (anti-theme)
   - key wound / fear
4. **Antagonist system**
   - motive and logic
   - methods
   - what they are willing to burn
5. **Stakes**
   - personal
   - relational
   - societal
6. **World rules**
   - constraints, costs, limits
7. **Structure choice**
   - Seven-Point map or Three-Act map
8. **Key turning points**
   - opening image
   - inciting incident
   - Plot Turn 1
   - Pinch 1
   - Midpoint shift
   - Pinch 2
   - Plot Turn 2
   - Climax
   - closing image
9. **Set pieces** (3–7)
10. **Series continuity hooks**

---

## 11) Revision System (Macro → Micro)

### 11.1 Macro revision passes
1. **Premise integrity:** does every major event serve the premise?
2. **Theme proof:** does the plot pressure-test the Lie/Truth argument?
3. **Causal chain:** does each event cause the next?
4. **Pacing:** are there saggy middles? Do peaks escalate?
5. **Payoff:** do promises made in Act I pay off in Act III?

### 11.2 Micro revision passes
- **Scene analysis:** goal, conflict, outcome, change.
- **Dialogue pass:** read aloud, tighten, add subtext.
- **Prose pass:** reduce filters, trim adverbs, increase sensory proof.
- **Line edit:** grammar and polish.

### 11.3 Denouement principle
Leave enough reflection space for catharsis, without diluting the ending.

# PART II — DARKWELL SERIES CANON MODULE

*(This section encodes the Darkwell universe rules as constraints an AI must not violate.)*

## 12) Darkwell Baseline Reality
- The world is our world, minimally bent.
- Geopolitics, intelligence agencies, modern tech, media cycles, and institutional dysfunction behave plausibly.
- Governments are fragmented, petty, and reactive. Competence varies.

## 13) The Three Systems of Power
1. **Nation-states:** legitimacy-driven, short-horizon, reactive.
2. **Immortal networks (Dominatus, Concordant, sleepers, sanctums):** long-horizon, historically scarred, risk-averse.
3. **Individuals with leverage:** people who can shift outcomes without commanding armies.

The story lives in the friction between these systems.

---

## 14) Noah Hammond (Darkwell) Prescience Rules
### 14.1 Nature of visions
- Non-volitional. Noah cannot summon them.
- Pattern-based, first-person fragments; may jump POV.

### 14.2 Constraints
- Noah is not omniscient.
- He cannot see events decided when he is dead.
- He cannot see events where another immortal is directly involved.

### 14.3 Obfuscation and decay
Visions degrade when:
- shared too widely
- reduced to bureaucratic summaries
- forced into conventional intel frameworks

### 14.4 Cost of intervention
Preventing catastrophe does not erase cost.
- harm transfers
- risk concentrates
- catastrophe delays into something worse

### 14.5 Oracle Veil skepticism
The program must remain skeptical.
- analysts downgrade certainty
- leadership second-guesses urgency
- politics distort action

Noah’s authority is never permanent. Every exception creates enemies.

---

## 15) Immortals: Biology and Limits
### 15.1 What they are
- Human-adjacent (Homo extremis)
- stop aging past maturity
- heal from most injuries, but regeneration is finite

### 15.2 How they die
- destruction of the brain (decapitation or equivalent)
- sustained damage beyond regenerative threshold (nuclear-scale, volcano-scale)

They can bleed out, be clinically dead for minutes, be mistaken for corpses.

### 15.3 Reproduction
- Immortal + immortal: ~95% sterile.
- Immortal + human: rare, unpredictable.
- Offspring are mortal, may have “echoes” (longevity, mental gifts).

### 15.4 Society rules
- Sanctums are neutral ground.
- Direct violence against immortal infrastructure is taboo.
- Mortals are not mass collateral unless existential stakes justify it.

Dominatus violates norms deliberately. That signals escalation.

---

## 16) Dominatus: Philosophy and Threat Model
- Not evil for evil’s sake.
- Core belief: mortals cannot steward the long term.
- They use stress tests and proof-of-concept attacks to demonstrate institutional failure.

---

## 17) Darkwell Thematic Engine
Core questions:
- Is intervention moral if it removes choice?
- Is inaction moral if you know the outcome?
- Who gets to decide what “stability” costs?
- Is the world worth saving if it never learns why it was saved?

---

## 18) Plausibility Anchors for Espionage-Tech Thrillers (Use Carefully)
When you need modern intel texture:
- Emphasize tradecraft as relationship, vetting, and leverage.
- Keep institutions fallible.
- Use tech as an amplifier of existing incentives, not magic.

Examples of “texture sources” you can borrow without derailing plausibility:
- surveillance detection routes (normal errands used as cover)
- modern compromise vectors (phones, TVs, cars as attack surfaces)

# PART III — READY-TO-USE AI PROMPT TEMPLATES

## 19) Outline Prompt (Seven-Point)
Paste into your AI tool:

```text
You are a professional novelist and story architect.

Framework: Dan Wells Seven-Point Structure.
Task: Build a Seven-Point map for my premise and cast. Output only the map.
Style: Crisp bullets, no filler.

Premise:
[PASTE]

Key characters:
[PASTE]

Constraints:
- Keep antagonist active (pinch points must be antagonist pressure).
- Midpoint must be a true shift from reaction to action.

Output format:
1) Resolution
2) Hook
3) Midpoint
4) Plot Turn 1
5) Plot Turn 2
6) Pinch Point 1
7) Pinch Point 2
```

## 20) Scene Draft Prompt (Goal-Conflict-Outcome)
```text
Identity: You are a line-by-line scene writer with strong action clarity.
Task: Draft one scene in third-limited past tense.
Framework: Goal → Conflict → Outcome (value shift).
Style: Concrete, lived-in, minimal exposition. Add 1–2 mundane frictions that affect behavior.

Series canon / rules:
[PASTE RELEVANT RULES]

Scene brief:
- POV: [NAME]
- Scene goal:
- Opposition:
- Stakes in this moment:
- Must include:
- Must not include:
- End on an open circuit:
```

## 21) Emotional Check Prompt
```text
You are a ruthless developmental editor.
Task: Analyze this dossier/outline for theme cohesion and emotional payoff.

Output:
1) Primary theme(s) explained in specific story terms
2) Secondary theme(s)
3) Where theme should appear but is weak
4) Moments that could become a gut-punch with small changes
5) A prioritized improvement plan (5–10 bullets)

Text:
[PASTE]
```

## 22) Logic and Plausibility Check Prompt
```text
You are a logic auditor for thrillers.
Task: Identify causal breaks, timeline problems, implausible tradecraft/tech, and convenience.

Output:
- Top 10 issues (severity-tagged)
- Suggested fixes that preserve the premise
- Continuity risks to track

Text:
[PASTE]
```

## 23) Rewrite-Only Prompt (Implement Plan Without Drifting)
```text
Task: Implement ONLY the changes in the improvement plan into the text below.
Rules:
- Do not add new plot lines.
- Do not change names unless explicitly requested.
- Preserve voice and existing good lines.

Improvement plan:
[PASTE]

Text to revise:
[PASTE]
```

## 24) Canonical Continuity Snapshot Template (Series Control)
Use this after every chapter draft.

```markdown
[SNAPSHOT — UPDATED AFTER CHAPTER X]
- Timeline (date/time, elapsed time)
- Locations (where each key character is)
- Status/Injuries (limits, meds, fatigue)
- Inventory/Props (gained/lost/used/broken)
- Obligations/Debts (owed, promised, blackmail, warrants)
- Secrets/Knowledge Map (who knows what changed this chapter)
- Relationship State (trust/leverage/conflict shifts)
- Active Threats (hunters, deadlines, surveillance, heat level)
- Open Loops (new or escalated)
```

---

## 25) Final “Cut the Author” Checklist
Run this on every chapter before locking.
- Remove lines that explain what behavior already shows.
- Replace at least one abstract “meaning” line with concrete action/sensation.
- Remove at least one adjective per paragraph unless it changes the action.
- Break accidental symmetry (repeated sentence templates, list-rhythms).
- Confirm action clarity: hands, objects, positions, cause-and-effect.
